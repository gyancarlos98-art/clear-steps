The problem isn't the delay

  • — You know by 7am that you won't make it, and you still haven't sent anything by 10.
  • — You write the message four times and it keeps sounding like an excuse.
  • — You said "I'll let you know" and then never closed the loop.

People generally absorb a missed deadline. What lands badly is finding out late, from someone else, with no idea what happens next. Silence is what gets read as unreliability — not the disruption itself.

The six steps

S — STATEThe minimum necessary situation. Not your medical history.
I — IDENTIFYThe commitment, time or system actually affected.
G — GIVE NOTICEThrough the correct channel, as early as is reasonable and safe.
N — NAMEWhat you can still do, and what you can't. Both.
A — ARRANGEThe next update or the repair step, with a time on it.
L — LOOP BACKUntil everyone affected knows the new status.

Where this guide stops

SIGNAL is about communication. Some situations are not communication problems, and treating them as one can cost you a protected right. The guide says so plainly and tells you where to go instead:

  • Serious health condition — yours or a family member's (FMLA, state leave)
  • A condition that needs an accommodation (that's an ADA process, not a phrasing problem)
  • Being treated worse after reporting something or asking for leave
  • Discrimination, harassment or safety
  • Being asked to sign something under pressure

When the issue is a right rather than a relationship, the right comes first. That's HR, an employment lawyer, or the relevant agency — not a better-worded message.
